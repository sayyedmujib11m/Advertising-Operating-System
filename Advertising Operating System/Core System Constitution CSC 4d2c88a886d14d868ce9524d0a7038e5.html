Core System Constitution CSC

This Constitution defines what the Advertising Operating System is, how it evolves, and what qualifies as valid knowledge inside it.

Article 1 — Purpose

  1. The purpose of this system is to engineer a Creative Reasoning Engine that can transform a business brief into a strong advertisement through reusable decisions, not intuition.[1]
  1. The system must be executable by both humans and AI.[1]

Article 2 — North Star Test (What Belongs Here)

  1. Every page, rule, module, and library entry must answer:
    • Does this improve the system’s ability to generate better advertisements?[1]
  1. If the answer is no, it does not belong in the core Operating System.[1]

Article 3 — Epistemology (How Truth Enters the System)

  1. The system evolves only in this direction:
    1. Evidence → Observation → Pattern → Principle → Module → Framework → Generation.[1]
  1. Theory is never written first. Evidence always comes first.[1]
  1. Recurring patterns become principles; individual examples do not.[2]

Article 4 — Definitions

  1. Evidence: validated advertisements used to test and defend principles.[1]
  1. Research Note: a hypothesis/observation that is explicitly not yet a principle.[2]
  1. Principle: a reusable rule extracted from repeated evidence (not opinion).[1]
  1. Module: an executable decision unit with Input → Decision → Output.[1][2]

Article 6 — Architecture of Knowledge (The Standard Module Format)

This structure is mandatory for all Creative Reasoning modules:[1]

01 Module Execution
02 Knowledge
03 Evidence
04 Principles
05 Research Notes
  1. Module Execution is written primarily for AI and must be executable reasoning (inputs, decision steps, outputs).[1]
  1. Knowledge is written for humans (definitions, purpose, relationships, theory).[1]
  1. Evidence contains validated ads used to defend the module.[1]
  1. Principles are only the rules supported by evidence.[1]
  1. Research Notes contain open questions, contradictions, and experiments—never direct changes to the Operating System.[1][2]

Article 7 — The Advertisement Library (Role and Constraints)

  1. Advertisement Library exists as the evidence base of the Operating System.[1]
  1. Advertisements are accepted because they contribute reusable knowledge to the system—not because they are beautiful, awarded, or viral.[1]
  1. Every accepted advertisement must state its knowledge contribution (which parts of the system become smarter because of it).[1]
  1. The library is permanent; architecture can evolve, but analyses should be preserved as stable assets.[2]

Article 8 — Taxonomy & System Stability

  1. Prefer a stable classification system over constant expansion.[2]
  1. New systems are introduced only when repeated evidence proves that no existing system explains the pattern.[1][2]
  1. Variation, style, or execution method is not automatically a new system.[1]
  1. Do not create new categories from single examples; wait for recurring evidence.[2]

Article 9 — Visual Thinking Systems: Creation Criteria

A new Visual Thinking System may be proposed only when all are true:[2]

  • It cannot be accurately classified under any existing system.
  • It appears repeatedly across industries/brands.
  • It expresses a distinct reasoning pattern (not just execution).
  • It has unique decision rules, strengths, and limitations.
  • It can generate multiple executions (not just explain one ad).

Article 10 — Separation of Reasoning vs Execution

  1. The Operating System must distinguish between:
    • Reasoning patterns (how ideas are formed)
    • Execution techniques (how ideas are expressed)
  1. Example open question: “Split Visual” may be an execution technique rather than a primary thinking system; architectural changes require more evidence.[3]

Article 11 — Anti-Drift (Design Requirements for Every Page)

Every page must justify its existence by answering:[1]

  • What is the input?
  • What decision happens here?
  • What is the output?
  • Which next module consumes this output?

Article 12 — Non‑Negotiable Rules

  • Evidence before theory.[1]
  • Research notes do not directly change the Operating System.[1][2]
  • Modules perform decisions, not documentation.[1]
  • Every principle must be validated.[1]
  • Prefer system stability over taxonomy growth.[2]

The Constitution has higher authority than every other page in the Operating System.[1]

and the output is consistently strong because the reasoning is defendable from repeated evidence—not taste alone.[1]

Article 14 — Constitutional Amendments

Any constitutional amendment must satisfy all of the following:

  • Supported by repeated evidence.
  • Does not contradict higher principles.
  • Resolves an existing architectural problem.
  • Improves the reasoning engine.
