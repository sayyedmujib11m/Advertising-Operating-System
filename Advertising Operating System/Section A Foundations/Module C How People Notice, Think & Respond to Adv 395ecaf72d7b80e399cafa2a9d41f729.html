Module C. How People Notice, Think & Respond to Advertisements


Before You Learn How to Create Better Advertisements, Learn How People Actually Think.
Every advertisement is ultimately an attempt to influence a human decision.

That person may be a shop owner looking for a CCTV system for his shop, a couple buying jewellery for their wedding, or a farmer considering a new water pump. Different people. Different needs. The common factor is that every advertisement competes for a few seconds of their attention.


The biggest mistake designers make is assuming people study advertisements.
They don't.
People notice, ignore, interpret and decide, often within moments.
If you don't understand that process, you're designing for yourself instead of the customer.


People Don't See Advertisements.
They Notice Patterns.


Imagine walking through a crowded market.
Hundreds of signboards surround you.
Can you remember all of them?
Probably not.


Your brain filters almost everything because processing every visual would be exhausting.
The same thing happens on social media.
Hundreds of posts.
Thousands of images.
Most disappear without entering conscious thought.
Your advertisement isn't competing against another advertisement.
It's competing against the brain's natural tendency to ignore unnecessary information.
The first challenge isn't persuasion.
It's being noticed.


Attention Is Earned, Not Given.


Designers often ask,
"How do I make my advertisement attractive?"
A better question is,
"Why would anyone stop looking?"
People notice things that are:
Different
Unexpected
Personally relevant
Emotionally meaningful
Visually simple
Easy to understand

Not because they're colourful.
Not because they're expensive to produce.
Because the brain believes they're worth processing.


The Brain Loves Shortcuts.


The human brain makes thousands of decisions every day. To save energy, it relies on shortcuts. Advertising takes advantage of those shortcuts.


When people see:
A lion.
They think strength.
A shield.
They think protection.
A broken chain.
They think freedom.
A heart.
They think love.


Nobody needs to explain these meanings. The brain has already learned them. This is why visual metaphors work.
They borrow existing knowledge instead of creating new knowledge. Good advertising doesn't fight the brain. It works with it.


Emotion often starts the decision. Logic usually justifies it.


Many products appear to be purchased logically. But look a little deeper. A customer may compare specifications before buying a mobile phone. But why did they start considering that phone in the first place?
Maybe they loved the camera.
Maybe they trusted the brand.
Maybe their friends recommended it.
Maybe it made them feel modern.


Logic often justifies a decision that emotion has already influenced. This doesn't mean facts are unimportant. It means facts become more persuasive when they're connected to a feeling.


Features Rarely Create Desire.


Consider two ways of advertising the same air conditioner.


Version One
1.5 Ton
5 Star Rating
Inverter Compressor
Copper Condenser
Everything is technically correct.
Everything is also emotionally neutral.


Now consider this.
"Walk into a cool home after spending all afternoon in the summer heat."
The product hasn't changed. The feeling has. Customers remember experiences far more easily than specifications.


Relevance Beats Creativity.


An advertisement can be brilliantly creative and still fail. Why?
Because creativity alone doesn't create interest. Relevance does.


A mother buying baby products notices different advertisements than a college student buying headphones.
A farmer notices different information than a software engineer.

People automatically pay more attention to problems they already have. Your job isn't to interrupt everyone.
Your job is to become relevant to someone.


Curiosity Is One of Advertising's Strongest Tools.


The brain dislikes incomplete information.
When something feels unfinished, unexpected or mysterious, we instinctively want to resolve it.
This is why techniques like Split Visuals, Negative Space and Shadow Metaphors are so effective.
They create a small moment of curiosity.
The viewer pauses.
Looks again.
Solves the visual.
That extra second dramatically increases the chance that the advertisement will be remembered.

Curiosity should never become the message. It should lead the viewer to the message.


Simplicity Reduces Mental Effort.


Every additional element asks the brain to process one more thing.
Another icon.
Another photograph.
Another badge.
Another colour.
Another font.
Eventually the viewer stops trying.
This is called cognitive overload.
A strong advertisement doesn't remove elements because minimalism is fashionable.
It removes them because every unnecessary element increases mental effort.
People naturally prefer messages that are easy to understand.


People Remember What They Can Associate.


Memory works through association.
We rarely remember isolated information.
We remember connections.
This is why advertising often connects products with familiar ideas.
A cheetah represents speed.
A mountain represents strength.
A lock represents security.
A sunrise represents a new beginning.
These associations already exist in people's minds.
The advertisement simply activates them.
The stronger the association, the easier the message becomes to remember.


Trust Is Built Before the Sale.


People rarely buy from brands they don't trust.
Trust can come from many sources.
Consistency.
Reputation.
Recommendations.
Quality.
Professional presentation.
Clear communication.
An advertisement cannot manufacture trust overnight.
But it can strengthen or weaken the trust that already exists.
Confusing advertisements reduce confidence.
Clear advertisements increase it.

People Buy When the Decision Feels Safe.

People often imagine buying decisions as logical calculations. In reality, uncertainty stops more purchases than a lack of information.

Every unanswered question creates friction.

Can I trust this brand?

Will this product last?

Is it worth the price?

What if I make the wrong choice?

Good advertising doesn't answer every possible question. It removes enough doubt for the customer to feel comfortable moving forward.

Customers don't always choose the objectively best product. They often choose the product that feels like the safest decision. The clearer the communication, the easier the decision becomes.


Every Decision Is a Balance.


Whenever someone sees an advertisement, their brain silently weighs two questions.
"Why should I care?"
and
"Is this worth my time?"
If the advertisement answers both quickly, the conversation continues.
If it doesn't, the viewer moves on.
The decision is rarely conscious.
It happens automatically.


What This Means for an Advertising Designer.


Understanding psychology doesn't mean manipulating people.
It means respecting how people naturally think.
Instead of forcing attention—
Earn it.
Instead of overwhelming—
Simplify.
Instead of listing features—
Communicate value.
Instead of making people work—
Make understanding effortless.
The best advertisements don't fight human psychology.
They align with it.


Practical Principles


Before developing any concept, ask yourself:
Why would someone notice this?
Why would they care?
What emotion am I trying to create?
Is the message immediately relevant to the audience?
Am I making the idea easier or harder to understand?
What existing association or memory can I use?
Is curiosity helping the message, or distracting from it?

If you can answer these questions honestly, you're no longer designing only with aesthetics in mind.
You're designing with human behaviour in mind.


Key Takeaways


Every advertisement competes for attention before it can communicate.
People notice relevance before beauty.
Emotion usually influences decisions before logic justifies them.
Curiosity increases attention when it supports the message.
Simplicity reduces mental effort.
Familiar symbols and associations make messages easier to remember.
Strong advertising works with human psychology instead of against it.
