Module B. Where Advertising Ideas Come From


The biggest myth in advertising is that good ideas arrive as moments of inspiration. They don't.


If that were true, agencies couldn't consistently produce campaigns week after week, across completely different industries.
The truth is much less romantic.
Great advertising ideas are usually the result of asking better questions.
When I review work from junior designers, I rarely see a lack of creativity.
I see a lack of investigation.
Most people jump from the brief straight into Photoshop.
Experienced creatives spend more time understanding the problem than designing the solution.
An idea is rarely invented.
More often, it's discovered.

---
Every Advertisement Starts With a Problem
A client doesn't walk into an agency because they need a poster.
They walk in because they have a business problem.
Sales are down.
A new product needs awareness.
A competitor has entered the market.
People don't understand the product.
Customers don't trust the brand.
The advertisement is simply one possible solution.
If you only think about the artwork, you're solving the wrong problem.

---
Don't Ask "What Should I Design?"
Ask:
"What am I trying to make people believe?"
That single question changes everything.
Imagine three different clients.
A jewellery store.
A hospital.
A solar company.
All three could ask for a festive social media post.
The request sounds identical.
The communication isn't.
The jewellery brand wants people to associate the festival with celebration and gifting.
The hospital wants to reinforce trust and care.
The solar company wants people to think about saving money and investing in the future.
The festival is the same.
The idea is different.

---
Every Great Idea Begins With a Single Sentence
Before searching for references, write one sentence.
> After seeing this advertisement, I want the customer to believe ____________.


Not buy.
Not click.
Believe.
Because actions follow beliefs.
Examples:
"I want customers to believe this cement lasts longer."
"I want customers to believe this jewellery makes celebrations more memorable."
"I want customers to believe this mobile phone has the best camera in its price range."
If you can't complete this sentence, you don't have a communication objective yet.

---
The Best Ideas Usually Come From Four Places
Over the years, I've noticed that most successful advertising concepts originate from one of these four sources.
1. The Product
Sometimes the product itself contains the idea.
A foldable phone.
A transparent bottle.
A diamond.
A pressure cooker.
Ask yourself:
What's unusual about this product?
Not every product has an interesting feature.
But every product has something worth exploring.

---
2. The Customer
Sometimes the product isn't interesting.
The customer is.
How do they think?
What frustrates them?
What excites them?
What are they afraid of?
A water purifier advertisement becomes more powerful when it starts with a parent's concern, not with a filtration diagram.

---
3. The Benefit
This is where many strong advertisements come from.
Don't advertise the feature.
Advertise what the feature changes.
A fast processor becomes uninterrupted gaming.
A waterproof watch becomes confidence in the rain.
An inverter battery becomes an uninterrupted family dinner during a power cut.
Benefits create stories.
Features create specifications.

---
4. Culture
Some ideas already exist in people's minds.
Festivals.
Traditions.
Cricket.
Monsoon.
Wedding season.
Examinations.
Local habits.
When an advertisement connects naturally with something people already understand, communication becomes easier.
That's why festival advertising can be so effective when the connection is genuine.
Not because there are flowers or diyas on the artwork.
Because the product becomes part of the cultural moment.

---
The Question Behind Every Good Advertisement
Whenever I feel stuck, I stop looking for layouts.
Instead, I ask questions.
What problem does this solve?
Why should anyone care?
What's different about it?
What's surprising about it?
What would happen if this product disappeared?
What's the first thing customers tell their friends after buying it?
Those answers are usually more valuable than hours spent browsing design galleries.

---
References Don't Give You Ideas.
They Give You Executions.
This is one of the easiest traps to fall into.
Open Pinterest.
Search "Jewellery Advertisement."
Find something attractive.
Recreate it.
The result might look professional.
It probably won't be original.
References are useful for understanding composition, lighting, typography and colour.
They are poor sources of ideas.
Ideas should come from the product, the customer and the message.
Not from another advertisement.

---
The Creative Process Is Not Linear
Beginners expect the process to look like this:
Brief
↓
Idea
↓
Design
↓
Done.
Real projects are rarely that neat.
The process usually looks like this.
Understand the brief.
↓
Research the product.
↓
Research the customer.
↓
Write the communication objective.
↓
Think of five bad ideas.
↓
One interesting thought appears.
↓
Develop it.
↓
Realise it doesn't work.
↓
Try another direction.
↓
Simplify it.
↓
Design it.
↓
Remove unnecessary elements.
↓
Present it.
↓
Refine it.
Good ideas aren't found because someone is naturally creative.
They're found because someone is willing to stay with the problem longer than everyone else.

---
Don't Chase Originality.
Chase Truth.
Many designers try to make every advertisement look unlike anything that's ever been created.
Originality is valuable.
But originality without communication is decoration.
A familiar idea communicated brilliantly will always outperform an original idea that nobody understands.
The goal isn't to surprise people.
The goal is to make them understand—and remember.

---
The Framework I Use Before Every Advertisement
Before sketching a single layout, I answer these questions:
What is the product?
↓
Who is the customer?
↓
What problem does it solve?
↓
What is the strongest benefit?
↓
What should people believe after seeing this advertisement?
↓
What visual idea communicates that belief most clearly?
Only after answering these questions do I start thinking about colours, layouts, typography or AI prompts.
By then, the design is no longer a search for an idea.
It's simply the execution of one.

---
One Habit That Changed My Work
Whenever I saw an advertisement I admired, I stopped asking:
"How did they design this?"
Instead, I asked:
"Why did they think of this?"
That single question taught me more about advertising than any software tutorial ever could.
Because great advertising isn't built on tools.
It's built on ways of thinking.

---
My critique of this page
This is much closer to the voice of an experienced Creative Director because it teaches how professionals approach problems, not just what they believe. It avoids dramatic "rules" and instead explains patterns that emerge from real agency work.
One thing I'd change in the structure of your handbook: after this page, I would not jump into your 17 concept techniques. The next page should be "Consumer Psychology", because once you understand where ideas come from, the next logical question is why certain ideas persuade people. Only after those foundations would I move into the visual concept systems like Split Visuals, Shadow Metaphors, and Shape-Based Concepts. That sequence mirrors how advertising ideas are actually developed in practice.
