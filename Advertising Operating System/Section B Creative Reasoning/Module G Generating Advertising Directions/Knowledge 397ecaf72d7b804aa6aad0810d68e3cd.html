Knowledge

Don’t Look for Ideas First.

Look for Directions.

One of the biggest misconceptions in advertising is that the first good idea you think of is the one you should design.

It rarely is.

Experienced creatives don’t begin by searching for concepts.

They begin by searching for directions.

A direction defines the conversation.

A concept defines how that conversation is expressed.

Understanding the difference is one of the biggest steps from being a graphic designer to becoming an advertising creative.

What Is A Direction?

A direction is a possible way of looking at the same product.

It is not a layout.

It is not a headline.

It is not a visual.

It is simply a communication path.

Imagine ten different people advertising the same jewellery store.

Every one of them may create a different advertisement.

Not because they have different design skills.

Because they chose different directions.

The direction always comes before the design.

One Product. Many Directions.

Consider this brief.

Product: Gold Jewellery
Occasion: Akshaya Tritiya
Offer: 0% Making Charges

Now ask yourself.

What conversation should this advertisement have?

Possible directions could be:

Invest in your future.

Celebrate tradition.

The perfect family gift.

The beginning of prosperity.

Pure gold. Pure trust.

The offer hasn't changed.

The product hasn't changed.

Only the communication direction has.

Why Most Designers Get Stuck.

Many designers open Pinterest before deciding what they want to communicate.

As a result, they start copying executions instead of creating ideas.

The real problem isn't a lack of creativity.

It's the absence of direction.

Without direction, every reference looks equally good.

Once the direction is clear, choosing references becomes much easier.

A Direction Is Not A Concept.

This is an important distinction.

Direction: "Security."

Possible concepts:

A CCTV camera watching over sleeping children.

A CCTV camera replacing a guardian.

A CCTV camera casting the shadow of a security guard.

Three different concepts.

One direction.

Directions create possibilities; concepts choose one.

Where Do Directions Come From?

Strong advertising directions usually emerge from one of these sources.

  • The Product

What makes the product different?

What is its strongest characteristic?

Sometimes the product itself provides the direction.

  • The Customer

What matters most to the customer?

What problem are they trying to solve?

What are they afraid of?

What are they hoping for?

Customer thinking often creates stronger directions than product thinking.

  • The Occasion

Festivals.

Seasons.

Life events.

National events.

Local culture.

Sometimes the timing itself suggests the direction.

  • The Emotion

Ask yourself.

What should people feel?

Relief.

Excitement.

Trust.

Pride.

Love.

Confidence.

Emotion is often the fastest path toward memorable advertising.

  • The Brand

Every brand has a personality.

Premium.

Affordable.

Reliable.

Modern.

Traditional.

Your direction should strengthen that personality, not contradict it.

Generate Quantity Before Quality.

One of the biggest mistakes creatives make is judging ideas too early.

Instead of searching for one brilliant direction...

Generate many ordinary ones.

Write ten.

Even if the first five feel obvious.

Creative thinking improves through exploration, not expectation.

The strongest direction often appears after the obvious ones have been exhausted.

Don't Fall In Love With The First Direction.

The first direction is usually based on what everyone already thinks.

The second explores alternatives.

The third challenges assumptions.

The fourth begins to surprise you.

Great advertising rarely comes from the first thought.

It often comes from the first thought you almost ignored.

Test Every Direction.

Before turning a direction into a concept, ask yourself:

Is it relevant to the product?

Does it support the communication objective?

Is it different from what competitors usually say?

Can it generate multiple concepts?

Would customers actually care?

If the answer to most of these questions is no...

Find another direction.

Example

Imagine a client says:

"We sell premium office chairs."

Possible directions:

  • Comfort during long working hours.
  • Better posture.
  • Professional workspace.
  • Health over productivity.
  • Work without fatigue.
  • Confidence during important meetings.

Each direction can produce completely different advertisements.

The direction determines the creative journey.

Direction Before Reference.

Only after choosing a direction should you begin collecting references.

Without direction, references influence your thinking.

With direction, references support your thinking.

This small difference often determines whether an advertisement becomes original or derivative.

What This Means For An Advertising Creative.

A direction is a decision.

It defines the conversation you’re about to have with the customer.

Everything that follows—

The concept.

The visual metaphor.

The composition.

The typography.

The colour palette.

Should reinforce that direction.

Never begin with execution.

Begin with the conversation.

Practical Principles

Whenever you begin a project, ask yourself:

What conversation should this advertisement start?

Can I think of at least five different directions?

Which direction is most relevant to the audience?

Which direction best supports the communication objective?

Which direction has the greatest concept potential?

Am I choosing a direction or copying an execution?

Never confuse the two.

Key Takeaways

Directions come before concepts.

Concepts come before execution.

One product can generate many different communication directions.

Great creatives explore multiple directions before choosing one.

References should support a chosen direction—not define it.

The quality of your concepts depends on the quality of your direction.
